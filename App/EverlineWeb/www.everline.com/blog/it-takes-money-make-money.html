It takes money to make money

20th January 2015 - Last updated at 14:03

The research below was designed to provide some insight into the cashflow issues faced by small business owners. How they would use extra working capital if available, the hurdles they face when trying to obtain it and the level of detriment to the business when they are unable to do so.

Receiving a business loan

We asked over 1000 small business decision makers how they would split a £50,000 loan throughout their business. The largest portion (10%) would go towards materials/tools/non-IT equipment.

Following that:

  • 9% of the loan would go towards improving office facilities
  • 8% would go towards purchasing IT equipment
  • 7% would go towards promotional marketing

We also found that 44% had applied for a loan through traditional lenders in the past and experienced difficulties. 15% said the process was too long and complicated and 6% said the money took so long to come through that their business was compromised.

Issues affecting business growth

A business is only able to grow when it has access to the right resources. When this is not the case a common theme of issues tends to arise. Half (49%) of our decision makers said that their business has cash flow problems at least a couple of times a year with 13% saying they have issues approximately once a quarter.

A lack or working capital was deemed to be the most detrimental issue when trying to grow their business with 42% saying it was detrimental or extremely detrimental.

Following a lack of working capital, a lack of skilled staff (34%) and unsatisfied clients (34%) are the next issues that are most detrimental to business growth.

A quarter (26%) agree that their business growth has been restricted due to lack of access to cash from traditional lenders or unwillingness from traditional lenders to loan to small businesses.

Borrowing money

As is often the case whether on a personal or business level, we found that when thinking about borrowing money in general, over a third (36%) of our small business decision makers  said owing money makes them feel uncomfortable. 15% are embarrassed to ask friends or family for a loan and 9% believe there is a stigma around borrowing money as a small business owner.

Despite this, 27% would prefer to take out a short term business loan to fulfil a specific need within their business and pay it back quickly and a quarter (24%) think borrowing is a

necessary part of being a business owner.

On average, £12,938 has been borrowed to fill a cash gap in the business with the most (£6,875) from the bank followed by £2,155 from an alternative finance lender. Of those that have borrowed money, 30% spent it on paying bills/wages whilst 22% purchased more stock and a further 22% upgraded IT equipment.

Are organisations adapting?

The attitude of financial organisations towards small business lending can have an enormous impact on whether or not a business succeeds. Under a third (29%) of our small business decision makers agreed that banks understand the shifting challenges that face small businesses in the UK - which was the highest percentage of all organisations shown. Following banks, 28% agreed that Central Government understand, 26% technology companies and 24% agree that alternative lenders do.

When considering whether the same organisations have changed their services, products and behaviour to support small and medium sized businesses in the last 3 years, a third (33%) agreed that the central government had, a quarter (25%) agreed that banks had and 1 in 5 (21%) agreed that alternative lenders had.

Access to cash

Having fast and convenient access to cash is vital to any small business owner across the board. Of our sample group just 56% feel that they have fast and convenient access to cash to support their business needs. 18-34 year olds appear to be at a distinct disadvantage with the number dropping to just 40% and increasing to 62% for those aged 55 and over.

Of those that do have fast and convenient access to cash, 58% accessed the cash through personal savings, a quarter (26%) through a high street bank and 10% through friends/family. Just 7% accessed the cash through an alternative finance lender.

In line with having fast access to cash, 55% think that having quick and convenient access is becoming more important to small businesses. Just 2% think it is becoming less important.

 

Expected set up times

Of all of the necessary activity when setting up a new business, decision makers believe the most time consuming to be securing a loan (7 days). This is perhaps in line with the finding that just 7% accessed cash through an alternative lender, typically a much faster option. They believe the least time consuming to be setting up a new bank account (2 days).
