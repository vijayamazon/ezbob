How to make sure you have a work-life balance

4th March 2014 - Last updated at 11:13

Many entrepreneurs and business owners aren't the 9-to-5 type. A workaholic spirit, while often useful for getting a fledgling business off the ground, could be bad news for your personal life. Read our tips about how to try restoring balance.

Leaving on time - at least once a week 

Do you work late more often than you leave on time? Staying at your desk for longer than 9-5 could lead to burnout and waning attention levels, and make it take longer than it ought to accomplish key tasks.

Setting yourself a time that you WILL leave your desk, and sticking to it at least once a week may help you to focus and achieve more in a shorter space of time.

Building down time into your day

If long work days are unavoidable, getting enough break might help you to keep your concentration sharp. If lunch breaks are sacrosanct you can get away from your desk and go for a short walk to try to boost energy levels. A change of scene is often all it takes to give you a fresh perspective on problems you've been worrying away at all day.

Managing your time well

It's easy as a business owner to feel overwhelmed with all the tasks clamouring for your attention. Better time management may help you get on top of things and avoid stress and unpleasant surprises.

Some key aspects of good time management can include:

  • Making a to-do list for your week or day, with all your tasks listed in order of priority. This could help reassure you you're spending your precious time on the right things.
  • If you're always distracted by a ringing phone or knocks at the door, setting aside time each day to be 'out of contact' might enable you to tackle tasks that need your concentration.
  • Thinking about whether to tackle one 'big unpleasant task' first each day could help set up a feeling of calm and control for the rest of the day.
  • Considering whether to delegate tasks you don't enjoy, aren't good at or just feel take up too much of your day, either to an employee or a specialist (eg a financial consultant).
  • Thinking about whether you limit your time on non-work distractions such as personal social networking.

Learning how to say no

If you're often feeling overwhelmed, you could be taking too much on. You might want to consider whether you say no to favours or tasks that unnecessarily eat up your time - and even to new commissions and clients if they aren't profitable enough to be worthwhile.

If you have more profitable work than you can handle with your current resources – it might be time to hire help.

Switching off when you leave work

If you're still thinking about work when you get home, you might find it useful to draw a mental line at the end of the day and use your commute to make an effort to mentally move between 'modes'.

Also think about whether to use the same devices (such as laptop or phone) for both work and pleasure – the urge to 'just check a few emails' in your off-time could be overwhelming.

These days many owners of smaller businesses work from home, which can make it even harder to switch off. If you can, consider whether to put your work area in a separate place in the house (in a spare bedroom or dedicated office) – and shut the door when you leave.
