Small business: the building blocks to the economy

12th January 2015 - Last updated at 15:58

As the infographic shows, small businesses are prevalent throughout the entire UK. In fact, the UK thrives on small businesses, and it is clear that their growth and success impacts our economy considerably. Although the majority of small businesses are situated in the affluent city of London (17.8%) and South East (16.16%),1 overall, small businesses employ 52% of employees3 across the UK.

The USA has a much larger area and population than the UK, but a slightly lower SME employment rate of 50%6. So, the UK has a higher percentage of employees working for SMEs, however, it does have a smaller percentage than some other countries. For example, China sees 84% of employees 8 in SMEs, Germany 62.7%5, Japan 69%7, and France at 63.9%4.

To further stress their importance, small businesses constitute 99.6% of all small businesses in the UK’s private sector, which equates to approximately 4.8 million. This means large companies and corporations only account for 0.4% of all the private sector businesses based here.

It seems only natural then that when it comes to financing and lending, small businesses are given a big opportunity here in the UK. Investing in small businesses enables them to be even more successful, promoting growth and development. Investment can cover hiring extra staff, training, opening in new locations, bulk orders of stock, advertising, marketing and more. However, as the statistics in the infographic highlight, for every £100 UK banks lend, only £26 goes to small businesses9, with the rest (£74) going to that 0.4% of non SMEs.

When looking at this investment of £26, on average, SMEs will use that to generate £28610 for the economy overall. The importance of SMEs in the UK economy should not be underestimated, as larger businesses will, on average, only generate £121 for every £26 of investment11. If small businesses received more investment, proportionally they would contribute more to the local economy. For example, an investment of £74 would generate a massive £814 for the local economy, whereas larger businesses would only generate £342 for every £74 invested.

Looking at the bigger figures, each SME received on average £23,000 in lending, whereas non-small business receives £16,500,000! Despite employing more people across the UK, small businesses can only hire one new person with this £23,000, whereas larger companies can hire over 700 new people.

Doesn’t it seem like the figures are all wrong? Our economy has struggled through the last ten years, experiencing a double dip recession and causing a lot of difficulty for businesses across the UK. With the knowledge that smaller businesses really can make a difference to the economy and give a lot back, it seems only natural that UK small business investment should be embraced.

A lack of funding from traditional lenders means small businesses aren’t getting the money they need, and attitudes towards bank loans are also unfavourable; 40% of small business have been refused a loan from high street banks12. Because of this, many small businesses believe they can’t apply for a loan because they are likely to be rejected, impacting their credit score, or that there is too much paperwork or admin involved. They may also think that interest rates are too high – or that it will take too much time before the money comes through.

Even with these issues, 8 out of 10 small business owners are not even aware that alternative funding options exist13. However there are alternative methods of funding readily available, from flexible lenders like Everline, who value the importance of small businesses and their impact on our economy. Based on the information, investing in small businesses across the UK will provide far more benefit to job seekers, local communities and business owners themselves.
