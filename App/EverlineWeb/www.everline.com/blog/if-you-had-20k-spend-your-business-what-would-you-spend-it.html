If you had £20,000 to spend on your business, what would you spend it on?

3rd February 2014 - Last updated at 15:14

No matter how small your business, there's always the opportunity to grow and change. Circumstances change, people change, and the business decisions you made when starting up may need to be revised in the light of experience.

With limited resources, you need to think carefully about the wisest ways to invest in growing your business.

Some key areas you could consider

  • Marketing. National newspapers and television spots don't come cheap, but online marketing can be cost-effective – you could send newsletters to your customers, or promote your website and make it more visible to search engines.
  • IT – a more powerful computer to work on, faster broadband, new/upgraded software or perhaps networking your office to let your employees share files and systems.
  • Premises – a larger, better-equipped or more prestigious location. For example, managed premises could provide office space with facilities such as a shared receptionist and meeting space included in the price.
  • Training – for yourself or your employees. This is unlikely to be cheap – and don't forget to factor in the lost work time while you're learning.
  • Advisors – a few hours of a professional's time to talk about your tax return or your current marketing plan could be worth its weight in gold.
  • Employees – a new junior to train up in your business. Don't forget that as well as their salary (at least minimum wage – currently £6.31 an hour) you'll have to pay employer's NI contributions and provide work equipment and training.
  • Research and product development – including market research, testing and checking out the competition, to try to ascertain whether your new ideas have a chance of success.
  • Stock – take advantage of offers and deals to bring in stock that you can sell at a healthy profit
Finding your priorities

You probably won't have unlimited funds to spread around – so you'll need to make some hard decisions about where to spend.

It'll depend on the type of business you run. For example, if you create innovative products you might benefit from spending on new product development, or perhaps new equipment or training so you can outpace the competition. You're less likely to need high-powered IT.

£20k to spend on your business, what would you spend it on?

A business dealing with customers face-to-face, or relying on walk-by trade, could benefit from impressive premises in a good location – whereas a self-employed person who works mainly online and by phone could work from home, but might want to invest in marketing instead.

A rapidly expanding business might do well to employ a junior, who can take some of the burden off as workloads grow, or hire an adviser to help manage increased legal and tax responsibilities.

It's up to you to explore what would make the biggest impact, and what is just a 'nice to have'.

Steer carefully

When you think about your business finances, don't forget that cashflow may need to be top of your considerations. Even businesses with plenty of assets and strong potential can fail if the cash dries up.

If investing leaves you with no financial leeway to service your debts – including unexpected bills - it's time to stand back and think again.

This is a guide only and should not be taken or relied upon as professional advice. You should always obtain professional advice before making any decisions which may affect your business.
