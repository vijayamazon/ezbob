Creating a loyalty scheme

16th December 2013 - Last updated at 09:51

Want to keep your customers coming back again and again? A loyalty scheme is one way to encourage long-term customer retention and set you apart from your competitors.

We look at what's involved and why you might consider running one.

It's not just big businesses that can benefit from customer loyalty schemes. Small firms which rely heavily on repeat business and customer retention can reap serious rewards too.

After all, the cost of bringing in new business – through advertising, for example - can be exorbitant compared to simply keeping hold of existing clients who already know your service or product, so increasing repeat purchases through loyalty schemes can considerably boost revenue.

The good news is that loyalty programmes don't have to be complicated or expensive. You can easily create a scheme to suit your particular business needs, but do think carefully about what you want to achieve. Are you looking to encourage regular buyers to spend more? Promote specific stock? Encourage more frequent purchases during low periods?

Setting up your scheme

Examples of well-known big brand loyalty schemes include Tesco's Clubcard, which rewards customers with points redeemable against their next shop, and Café Nero, which gives regular buyers a free coffee after their ninth purchase or 'stamp'.

For small firms, it's relatively straightforward to follow these ideas, and there are no real rules on what rewards to offer – simply weigh up whether a fixed discount, gifts or other 'perks' like free delivery will be better appreciated by customers and more worthwhile for your business.

One simple scheme set up by Nottingham-based dog groomers Zoolittles gives regular customers a fifth pet grooming session for half price, plus 10 per cent off for anybody making a referral. Diners at Indian Summer restaurant in Brighton can redeem loyalty stamps for free food and drink, while visitor attraction Tulleys Farm in West Sussex has signed up to national loyalty programme Ice, to reward shoppers with points to spend at stores like John Lewis.

Remember that rewards or discounts don't always have to be related to your product. To add value, consider a reciprocal tie-up with another company - a sports retailer could team up with a local gym, for instance, for discounted memberships. 

Tips for creating a loyalty scheme

What is important is to ensure your loyalty scheme is simple to understand. Crucially, avoid making rewards either too trivial for customers to be interested or too costly to make it worthwhile.

Understanding customer behaviour

It's a good idea to trial your new loyalty scheme for a fixed period to evaluate its worth. Is it bringing in repeat business or boosting sales?

Information you collect from customers about their purchasing preferences can also help you target your marketing, so it's important to regularly track your data. By highlighting what frequent customers like to buy, loyalty schemes can improve your product ranges and stock too.

You must let customers know how you'll use their data in the terms and conditions when they sign up to the scheme, and make sure you keep any personal data secure.

Do's and Don'ts

  • DO think what the loyalty scheme is worth to you - for instance, the value of repeat purchases compared to the discount the customer gets.
  • DO publicise your scheme via your website, customer newsletters and social media.
  • DON'T make things too complicated – customers should clearly understand the benefits of signing up.

This is a guide only and should not be taken or relied upon as professional advice. You should always obtain professional advice before making any decision which may affect your business.
